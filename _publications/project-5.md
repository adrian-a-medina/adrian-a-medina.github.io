The Population Reference Bureau reported a rise in the proportion of American children ages 3-17 who experience anxiety/depression, going from 9% in 2016 to 12% in 2020. Resting-state functional connectivity (RSFC) is a promising technique for studying brain connectivity in child anxiety/depression, however, much remains to be understood about the connectivity between large-scale brain networks as biomarkers for early detection/intervention. To address this, the current study will use neuroimaging data from the Adolescent Brain Cognitive Development Study to examine how oscillations in the amygdala’s BOLD activity, and RSFC between the amygdala and the default mode and salience networks, respectively, predict the onset/maintenance of mental disorders in sub-clinical adolescents. Probing the distinctive connection patterns seen in this group, the findings of this study will be useful in developing interventions for pediatric anxiety and mood disorders, which is important for minimizing the detrimental effects of psychiatric symptoms/conditions in children.
