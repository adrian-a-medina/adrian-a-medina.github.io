Recent research has highlighted the importance of understanding the neural mechanisms that support early language development. This study examined the relationship between cortical volume in specific brain regions and language ability in infants and toddlers, and how this relationship is moderated by socioeconomic status (SES). A total of [insert number] children between [insert age range] years old participated in the study. Cortical volume in the left Medial Temporal Gyrus, Superior Temporal Gyrus, Inferior Frontal Gyrus, Angular gyrus, Supramarginal gyrus, and inferior parietal lobule was positively correlated with both expressive and receptive language scores, but the relationship differed for each brain region. Specifically, cortical volume in the left Inferior Frontal Gyrus had a stronger positive correlation with expressive language than with receptive language, while cortical volume in the left Superior Temporal Gyrus, Angular gyrus, Supramarginal gyrus, and inferior parietal lobule had a stronger positive correlation with receptive language than with expressive language. Moreover, the positive correlation between cortical volume and language ability was found to be weaker for individuals with lower SES levels. These findings have important implications for understanding the brain-behavior associations in early language development and the potential for early interventions to improve language outcomes for at-risk populations.
